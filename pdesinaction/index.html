PDEs in Action

from multi-scale to inverse problems

Dates & Venue 22nd to 24th June, 2026

Department of Mathematics

Conference Goal

To bring together leading researchers to discuss the analytical, computational, and applied aspects of multi-scale phenomena, fostering collaboration and advancing research in homogenization and related areas.

Focal Themes

Homogenization is a mathematical framework used to analyze the behavior of multi-scale media. It provides a way to approximate macroscopic properties of a heterogeneous media by an equivalent homogeneous model, playing a crucial role in composite materials, metamaterials, and porous media.

Theoretical aspects of Homogenization

The asymptotic analysis of solutions to partial differential equations (PDEs), leading to limit equations that describe the effective or bulk behavior of the medium, free from microscopic heterogeneities. These limit models are not only easier to solve but also capture the essential macroscopic features of the material.

Numerical methods for Homogenization

Developing efficient computational methods for approximating effective coefficients, solving limit equations, and simulating multi-scale behavior.

Control problems in multiscale setting

Understanding how systems governed by PDEs can be steered towards desired states, and how control strategies can be optimized in multi-scale settings.

Inverse problems in Homogenization

Reconstruction of fine-scale information or material properties from macroscopic data, which has practical implications in imaging, non-destructive testing, and material design.

    Regularization of Backward Heat Conduction Problem - A time fractional approach

    It is well known that the “backward heat conduction problem (BHCP)”, namely, the problem of determining the heat profile u(·, t) for 0 < t < τ from the knowledge of the final temperature u(·, τ ), is an ill-posed problem. In order to obtain stable approximations for this problem, many regularization methods are available in the

    literature. In this talk we shall consider a new regularization based on the time-fractional analogue of BHCP, devised by observing the crucial fact that the α-time-fractional BHCP for each 0 < t < τ and for each 0 < α < 1 is a well posed problem, and the corresponding solution u_(α)(·, t) satisfies []u_(α)(·, t) − u(·, t) [] _(L)2 → 0 as α → 1.

    Prof. Hari Varma

    The Role of PDEs in Medical Optical Sensing and Imaging

    Light propagation in media ranging from transparent optical elements to highly scattering biological tissue is governed by a hierarchy of mathematical models based on differential equations. This talk will provide an overview of light transport across these regimes, beginning with the electromagnetic wave equation and progressing to diffusion-based descriptions of multiple scattering in tissue. Particular empha-sis will be placed on the photon diffusion equation, which underpins near-infrared spectroscopy and related diffuse optical techniques for non-invasive tissue monitor-ing. The formulation of forward and inverse problems associated with these models will be discussed, with applications to the recovery of tissue optical properties and oxygenation. The talk will also introduce diffuse correlation spectroscopy, a related technique that exploits temporal fluctuations of laser speckle patterns to quantify blood flow in deep tissue. Examples will be drawn from biomedical optics applica-tions, including non-invasive monitoring of cerebral blood flow and tissue hemody-namics in human subjects. The aim is to illustrate how differential equations and inverse problem methodologies underpin modern optical approaches for functional tissue imaging.

    Prof. Antonio Gaudiello

    Stokes flow with injection in a thin T-like shaped structure

    In my talk I will present a paper written in collaboration with Renata Bunoiu (Universit´e de Lorraine - France) where we consider the steady incompressible flow of a Newtonian fluid injected in a thin two-dimensional T-like shaped structure and also subjected to an external force. We study the asymptotic behavior of such a problem when the small parameter describing the thickness of the branches of the structure vanishes. We derive the limit problem satisfied by the limit velocity and the limit pressure. We obtain a flux coupling condition due to the injection. We prove that the limit pressure is defined and continuous on the T-graph underpinning the initial thin structure. If the external force depends only on the variable along the branches, we explicitly calculate the solution to the limit problem. Moreover, if the external force is zero, we obtain a Kirchhoff type relation for the derivatives of the pressure at the junction of the T-graph.

    Prof. Raju K. George

    Mathematical Theory of Controllability

    In this talk, an overview of the mathematical theory of control systems, and in particular, their controllability properties will be discussed. We will begin by find-ing the solution of a control system using transition matrix and then analyze the controllability of linear systems. The analysis is done using tools from functional analysis, operator theory and monotone operators. After establishing the theory for finite dimensional linear systems, we will focus on non-linearly perturbed systems, namely the semi-linear systems. Controllability analysis of semi-linear systems is established using the fixed point theory and monotone operator theory. The dis-cussion on finite dimensional systems will further generalized to infinite dimensional systems, which come from partial differential equations. Here the notion of ap-proximate controllability and exact controllability are different which is not the case when dealing with finite dimensional systems. Exact controllability of thermo-elastic system is also established.

    Prof. S. Sundar

    High-Resolution Meshfree Method for Continuum Crowd Models with Applications to Evacuations During Flood Inundation and Smoke Propagation

    A profound understanding of the human motion dynamics in a large built environ-ment during mass gatherings is essential in comprehending emergency evacuation interventions and safety- related protocols. Recent advancements in empirical ob-servations of human psychological responses have revealed that the governing rules of human behavioral aspects can not only be predicted with adequate deterministic precision but also forged into mathematical formulations. These modeling studies enable crisis managers with reliable simulation tools to gain insights into critical facets of crowd disasters and enhance their decision-making abilities to facilitate safe egress in emergency situations.

    However, an accurate description of intelligent living systems such as humans requires a rigorous mathematical modeling theory, which not only exhibits qualita-tively consistent results but also replicates some of the inherent key features, such as self-organization, decision- making capability, and nonlinear interactions. The macroscopic scale of representation in this context is often invoked to comprehend large-scale collective human pattern formation in vast built environments, owing to its computational viability. The associated governing equations are typically constructed in a system of hyperbolic conservation law form, and the successful implementation of such models in real-world complex geometric configurations ne-cessitates precise computational algorithms that are numerically stable, reasonably efficient, and robust. This work puts forward a high-resolution, shock-capturing

    meshfree particle method in an Eulerian framework for the numerical approxima-tion of several widely adopted macroscopic pedestrian flow models. Then to study the collective behavior of crowds in a flooded evacuation scenario, we combine a macroscopic description of a crowd evacuation model with a hydrodynamic model of flood inundation described by a two-dimensional depth-averaged Shallow Water Equation (SWE), considering two-way dynamic coupling. Finally, a multi-group continuum crowd evacuation model is presented to simulate the emergent behavior of crowds from a smoke-impacted space.

    Keywords Macroscopic pedestrian model; Crowd evacuation; Collective behav-ior; Traffic instability; Stop-and-go waves; Meshfree method; Geometric conserva-tion; Flood evacuation; Coupled flood-crowd model; Multi-group continuum model; Smoke propagation; Behavioral dynamics in smoke

    Prof. K. Balachandran

    On the Solvability of Fractional Partial Differential Equations

    Many physical and biological problems can be modeled by partial differential equations, integral equations, or integro-differential equations. Recently, many mod-els have been reformulated and expressed in terms of fractional differential equations so that their physical meaning can be incorporated into the mathematical models more realistically. In fact, fractional calculus attracts many physicists, engineers, bi-ologists, and mathematicians because of its interdisciplinary applications. Therefore, it becomes important to study the existence and qualitative behavior of solutions to such equations in infinite-dimensional spaces. An attempt has thus been made to extend the existence results for partial differential equations to fractional partial differential and integro-differential equations in abstract spaces.

    The main aim of this talk is to discuss the existence results and solution represen-tations of fractional partial differential and integro-differential equations, including abstract fractional differential equations. It is shown that resolvent operators are more useful than semigroups of operators in dealing with abstract fractional differ-ential equations.

    Prof. Venky Krishnan

    Ray transform of symmetric tensor fields on Riemannian manifolds with conjugate points

    In this talk, we consider microlocal properties of the geodesic ray transform of symmetric m-tensor fields on Riemannian manifolds with boundary allowing the possibility of conjugate points. In the presence of conjugate points, under the as-sumption of no singular conjugate pairs, it is known from earlier works that the normal operator can be decomposed into a sum of a pseudodifferential operator and a finite number of Fourier integral operators. In 2-dimensions, we explicitly compute the principal symbols of these operators. We construct a parametrix recovering the solenoidal component of the tensor field modulo FIOs, and prove a cancellation of singularities result.

    Prof. Harsha Hutridurga

    Eigenvalue problems in perforated domains — periodic and random

    This talk focuses on eigenvalue problems — for the Laplacian, Elasticity, Bi-Laplacian, and Stokes operators — posed in perforated domains. We begin by revisiting classi-cal results for periodically perforated domains, drawing on the works of Vanninathan and Nandakumaran, and adopt the perspective of homogenization theory to derive asymptotic expansions for the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions in terms of a small parameter representing the perforation size.

    We then turn to the setting of randomly perforated domains, where the hole radii and positions are random. In this context, we highlight the role of the 2-capacity of sets in understanding the asymptotic behaviour of eigenvalues when the perforations are critically scaled.

    This talk is based on joint works with Chetan Pahlajani (IIT Gandhinagar), Manas Rachh (IIT Bombay), and Kshitij Sinha (IIT Bombay).

    Prof. Tuhin Ghosh

    An inverse homogenization problem for the Maxwell equations

    Abstract: The method of transformation optics has enabled the design of elec-tromagnetic parameters that cloak a region from external observation. However, these constructions are singular and anisotropic, making practical implementation difficult. Thus, regular approximations to these cloaks have been constructed that cloak a given region to any desired degree of accuracy. In this talk, we discuss how to construct isotropic approximations to these regularized cloaks using homogenization techniques.
